The 25 questions that change everything.

Most AI tutors treat every student the same. A good teacher doesn't. Here's how AspiremsAI builds — and continuously updates — a psychometric persona for every student, starting with 25 questions and refining it over every interaction.

Shambhavi Innovation Lab 19 April 2026 9 min read

A good teacher knows you.

She knows your name, sure. But more than that — she knows whether you're the kind of student who gets energised by being challenged or quietly demoralised by it. She knows you learn better from diagrams than paragraphs. She knows you compare everything to cricket. She knows you've been sleeping badly because your parents are arguing, and so today is not the day to push back on your answer to question four.

She uses all of that, in real time, without thinking about it. And she teaches you differently than she teaches the student sitting next to you.

Can an AI do this?

The difference between an AI that answers questions and an AI that knows who is asking is not incremental. It's categorical.

We think yes — but it requires more than a chatbot. It requires a persona. This essay walks through how AspiremsAI builds one, what framework sits under it, and the things it deliberately refuses to do with it.

OneWhy 25 questions?

Every adaptive system needs a calibration floor — some minimum amount of information before it can start meaningfully adapting.

Too few questions, and the starting persona is noise. The AI's attempts to personalise make arbitrary assumptions. Students notice. Trust erodes.

Too many questions, and you don't get an answer at all. We tested onboarding batteries of 10, 25, 50, and 75 questions in pilot schools. Completion rates at 75 questions dropped below 40%. At 25 questions, completion was above 85%. At 10, completion was higher still — but the resulting persona was too thin to be useful.

25 was the Pareto point. The calibration is strong enough to meaningfully adapt, and students actually finish the assessment.

We didn't invent a new battery. That's a red flag in any serious psychometric product — "We developed our own proprietary framework" usually means "We didn't want to pay licensing fees or do the research to validate against standard instruments." We built on decades of validated psychometric research. Every sub-score in the AspiremsAI persona maps to a published framework.

TwoThe five dimensions.

The 25 questions measure five psychological dimensions, each with well-established scientific foundations:

1. Personality — Big Five (OCEAN). Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism. The most widely validated personality framework in psychology, developed over decades by researchers including Costa & McCrae and Lewis Goldberg1. Five traits that reliably predict learning style, motivation patterns, and social behaviour. For AspiremsAI, Big Five helps us decide whether a student thrives on gamification (high Extraversion), needs structure (high Conscientiousness), or gets more from open-ended exploration (high Openness).

2. Learning style — VARK. Visual, Auditory, Reading/Writing, Kinesthetic. Neil Fleming's framework2. It tells us how content should be presented to this student. A strongly visual learner gets diagrams first. A reading-writing-oriented student gets textual explanations. A kinesthetic learner gets interactive simulations. VARK is contested in some education research — some studies find learning-style effects smaller than practitioners assume — but we treat it as a soft preference signal rather than a hard rule, and the combined effect with the other four dimensions is well-validated in our pilots.

3. Motivation type. Intrinsic vs extrinsic. Goal-orientation vs process-orientation. This draws from self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan)3. It tells the AI how to encourage — milestone markers and badges for the extrinsically motivated, deep-dive content and "why" questions for the intrinsically curious.

4. Stress management style. Active vs avoidant coping. Cognitive regulation vs emotional regulation. How the student responds to academic pressure. This tells the AI when to activate Gentle Mode — pulling back on challenge, offering breathing exercises, changing tone — and when pushing harder is actually what the student wants.

5. Social skills profile. Comfort with peer interaction, help-seeking behaviour, group dynamics preference. Tells the AI how to model collaborative learning — some students learn best from peer comparison, others are demotivated by it.

ThreeThe 18 sub-categories.

Within the five dimensions, we measure 18 more specific sub-categories. The combination produces an 18-dimensional "persona vector" — the structured representation of the student that sits alongside every AI response.

The reason for this granularity: the top-level dimensions correlate strongly but aren't identical, and students need different responses for different sub-dimensions. Some examples:

  • Within Conscientiousness, we measure persistence (follow-through on difficult tasks) and organisation (systematic approach) separately. They're correlated, but a student can have high persistence and low organisation. Each suggests a different kind of scaffolding.
  • Within Motivation, we differentiate ambition (outcome focus — "I want to become a doctor") from curiosity (process enjoyment — "I want to understand why"). An ambitious student thrives with milestone tracking. A curious one thrives with the "rabbit hole" — allowing deep dives.
  • Within Stress, we distinguish exam anxiety from general anxiety from social anxiety. Each responds to different AI behaviours. Exam anxiety is addressed with confidence-building micro-exercises; general anxiety with supportive tone and reduced cognitive load; social anxiety with reduced emphasis on peer comparison.
  • Within Learning style, the four VARK modes aren't equally weighted — we track dominant vs secondary styles separately, because most learners have a primary preference plus one backup mode that they'll accept.

FourHow the persona rebuilds itself.

A static persona would be useless. People change. Moods shift. Learning preferences evolve as topics get harder or easier. A Class 9 student who started with high confidence in Math may develop math anxiety by Class 11 if they hit a sustained series of difficult topics.

Every interaction updates the persona vector:

  • Every quiz answered contributes to the ability estimate for that subject and to the sub-category scores (persistence if they retry a wrong answer, curiosity if they explore related topics)
  • Every chat with the AI tutor contributes to language preference, interest signals, and — via sentiment analysis — emotional state trends
  • Every journal entry contributes to stress and social signals (with strict privacy — the content is never shown to staff, only the derived mood signal)
  • Every assignment completion contributes to conscientiousness sub-scores and to learning-style preferences (did they skip the video and read the text?)

The update function isn't retraining. The underlying AI model stays fixed. What changes is the persona vector that the AI reads before generating each response. That's why we can update continuously at low cost.

After two weeks of regular use, the AI typically knows that Aarti — to use a specific example from our pilots — thinks in analogies drawn from cricket. After four weeks, it knows she retains better with spaced practice than massed practice. After six weeks, it knows she gets anxious before parent-teacher meetings and automatically softens its tone that week.

Critically, every student's persona is tenant-isolated. Aarti's data stays with Aarti's school. It never trains a shared model that other students benefit from. Personalisation and privacy are reconciled by keeping the persona vector local to each tenant.

FiveWhat the persona does NOT do.

As much as we've said about what the persona enables, it's worth being equally explicit about what it refuses to do.

It does not label the student. The output of the 25 questions is not a diagnosis or a classification. Aarti is not "INTJ" or "Type A" or "visual learner." She is a set of weighted preferences on 18 dimensions, and every one of those weights can move over time. The persona is a set of dials, not a box.

It is not shared with parents or teachers. The persona vector is AspiremsAI's internal model of how to teach this student. It is not broadcast. Parents see academic progress. Teachers see classroom performance. The persona stays with the student and with the product.

It is not permanent. Every inference the AI makes from the persona can be overridden by the student directly. If the AI is giving you too many cricket analogies and you don't want them, you can say so, and it updates. If you want more challenging content than the persona suggests, you can request it. The persona serves the student, not the other way around.

It is not medical. If a student shows signs of severe distress, that's handled by the crisis detection system — a different architectural layer with different purpose and different data flow. The persona is about how to teach. The crisis system is about how to help. We deliberately keep them separate, because conflating them would mean every emotional signal gets treated as a teaching signal, which is the wrong response.

SixWhy this matters.

The students who thrive with human mentoring don't do so because their tutors know more than other tutors. They thrive because their tutors know them.

A one-on-one tutor in Kota, charging twenty thousand rupees a month, offers two things that group classes can't: responsiveness and recognition. The tutor notices which topics the student is avoiding. They remember that the student was confident last week and isn't today. They adjust.

That kind of attention has never been available at scale. Not because the pedagogy is mysterious — we've understood adaptive teaching for decades — but because the human labour required to do it for millions of students at once has never existed.

AspiremsAI is our attempt to make that kind of attention available to every student. Not just the ones whose families can afford twenty-thousand-rupee-a-month tutoring. Every student in a government school in Bihar. Every student in a tier-three private school in Vidarbha. Every student who has been, until now, taught as if they were a statistic.

SevenTry it for yourself.

The full 25-question assessment takes about seven minutes. It produces a starting persona. From there, every word the student says makes it more accurate.

Over a semester, we hope the AI knows the student almost as well as a good teacher would — better, perhaps, than a teacher managing forty other students possibly can. Not because AI is wiser than humans. Because AI has the time that humans, at 1:40, don't.

Schools piloting AspiremsAI can book a walkthrough at aspirems.ai, or write to info@shambhavilabs.com with "persona demo" in the subject line. We'll show you Aarti's persona dashboard (with identifying details removed) so you can see what the system actually looks like in use.

And come back to us in a month, or six months, once you've been using it. The persona won't be the same by then. That's the whole point.
