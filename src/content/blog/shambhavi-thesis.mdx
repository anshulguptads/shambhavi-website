<p class="lead">There is a paradox at the heart of how AI works in India. The best AI systems on Earth are built, trained, and maintained in large part by Indian engineers. India's technology services industry — TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL, Cognizant, and the global capability centres of every major Fortune 500 — exported more than eight billion dollars in software testing services alone in FY 2024–25<sup class="fn"><a href="#s1">1</a></sup>. Indian talent runs substantial parts of the AI organisations at Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, Meta, and Anthropic. We are, by any reasonable measure, one of the two or three countries on Earth most central to the creation of modern AI.</p>

And yet — for the overwhelming majority of Indians, AI is something they read about, not something they use. The language alone keeps most of the country out: the cheapest widely-owned phone in India doesn't fluently run ChatGPT in Hindi, let alone Bhojpuri or Odia. The tier-three engineering college isn't teaching with Copilot. The enterprise HR team is still fighting Excel exports from an HRMS built a decade ago for American labour law and retrofitted for Form 16.

This isn't about access to technology. It's about access to **excellence**.

> The finest teachers, the most sophisticated workplace tools, the most capable AI-engineering environments — these have always existed in India. They've only existed in small pockets.

A handful of IITs. A handful of Fortune 500 enterprises. A handful of senior engineers. Everyone else has been working with translated, retrofitted, or absent versions of the tools the best Indians already have.

Our thesis is that AI can be the great leveller — but only if someone is willing to build it for how India actually works. Not translated. **Built.** That's what Shambhavi Innovation Lab is.

## One thesis, at every stage of a life.

When we started sketching products in 2023, we weren't trying to find a single vertical to win. We were trying to answer a specific question: *across the life of an Indian person who wants to learn, work, and build — where are the gaps?*

At every stage, we saw the same pattern. World-class tools existed. They weren't available to most people. AI-native products were being built. They weren't being built for India first.

So we set ourselves the work of building four products, one for each major phase of the arc. School. University. Work. Engineering craft. Not because we want to be everything to everyone — we don't — but because the thesis holds equally well at each stage. And a single company, applying a consistent set of design principles, can move faster across these four markets than four separate companies each solving for one.

Here's what each product is for, what the evidence says about the gap it addresses, and why we chose the hard version of the problem.

## AspiremsAI, and the 1:7,300 problem.

In 2023, **13,892 students in India died by suicide**<sup class="fn"><a href="#s2">2</a></sup>. That is thirty-eight students a day. Every day. The number has been rising.

Consider the structure of the guidance system meant to catch them. In late 2025, CBSE announced that from 2026, every affiliated school must have a dedicated counsellor — at a 1:500 ratio<sup class="fn"><a href="#s3">3</a></sup>. Read that again: an aspirational 1:500, being introduced in 2026, applying only to CBSE schools. In most government schools, the actual ratio sits closer to 1:7,300. A 2015 study of 101 schools in Karnataka found that nineteen had any counsellor on their rolls at all<sup class="fn"><a href="#s4">4</a></sup>. The reality on the ground, a decade later, has not meaningfully changed.

Career guidance is almost non-existent — especially outside the metros. A ninth-grader in Patna choosing between Science, Commerce, and Arts will, in most cases, make that decision without meeting a single person whose job it is to help them think about it. That decision shapes the rest of their working life.

The obvious response, and the one most Indian edtech has taken, is to translate American products. Khan Academy in Hindi. Coursera in Tamil. Duolingo with a Bollywood owl. We don't think that's the right response. Translation doesn't solve the core problem, which is that the products weren't designed for the student we're trying to reach.

[AspiremsAI](/platforms/aspirems) is designed, from first principles, for:

- A student on a ₹5,000 Android phone — not a laptop
- Voice-first, because reading long English passages is slow when the student thinks in Hindi, Bengali, or Tamil
- An AI that knows their personality, their ambition, and how they learn — not a generic tutor
- Crisis detection that reaches a counsellor in under two seconds, because the data is clear on what's needed
- Privacy the student can trust — their journal entries never visible to parents or teachers, only distress signals

This isn't Khan Academy for India. It's what you build when you start from the Indian student and work outward.

## NeuroLink v2, and the one-million-person shortfall.

India's engineering pipeline admits roughly **3.9 lakh CS students every year**<sup class="fn"><a href="#s5">5</a></sup>. A well-known minority of them — the IITs, top NITs, a handful of private universities — receive world-class instruction. The rest get something else. Outdated curriculum. Overworked professors. Labs without modern compute. Twenty-five-year-old textbook references for a field that redefines itself every six months.

This wouldn't matter, except that India is simultaneously racing into an AI talent crisis of a scale almost no other country faces.

> Bain & Company projects 2.3 million AI job openings in India by 2027, with only 1.2 million qualified professionals — a shortfall of over one million people.

AI-related job postings have grown **21% annually since 2019**<sup class="fn"><a href="#s6">6</a></sup>. Compensation for these roles is rising at 11% a year. The Indian AI software market is expected to compound at 25–35% CAGR through 2027<sup class="fn"><a href="#s7">7</a></sup>. The demand side is exploding. The supply side isn't keeping up.

The math doesn't work. India cannot produce an AI workforce from 3.9 lakh admissions per year if only the top ten percent of institutions can actually teach the subject. And the obvious fix — hire IIT-grade faculty for the four hundred and fifty other engineering colleges — is impossible. The faculty don't exist. The institutions can't afford them if they did.

[NeuroLink v2](/platforms/neurolink) is what happens when you take that math seriously. Instead of trying to hire better professors, you build an AI layer that makes the existing lecture material interactive, personalised, and deeply probing. A state engineering college with an overworked professor doesn't need a better professor. It needs a system that takes her existing slides and turns them into a 24/7 AI tutor — one that keeps a living knowledge graph of every concept each student has and hasn't mastered, and closes the gap one adaptive challenge at a time.

Concept DNA, Ghost Mode, Paper-to-Playground, Challenge Arena — all serve a single goal: close the teaching-quality gap between the best 10% of Indian CS colleges and the other 90%. Not by bringing the 90% down to a more reasonable standard. By bringing them up.

## SanaDeep, and the colonised HRMS market.

The Indian enterprise HRMS market has been colonised — not by Indian companies, but by global products designed for American compliance and retrofitted for Indian regulations. The ones that have reached scale locally did so by aggressively building Indian features on top of architectures that were never designed for them.

The problem with retrofitting is that the hard things become afterthoughts. DPDPA 2023. Provident Fund. ESI. Gratuity. Form 16. The UAE's Wage Protection System. Arabic right-to-left for the Gulf workforce. In any HRMS that serves the markets [SanaDeep](/platforms/sanadeep) is built for, these aren't edge cases — they are the foundation.

An AI-native HRMS that treats Indian statutory compliance as an afterthought is a worse product than a legacy HRMS that treats it as the foundation. We took the other trade. SanaDeep is built AI-first **and** India-first. Nineteen modules. Hundreds of AI-powered features. Statutory payroll engines for India, UAE, UK, and US — tested, not hopeful. Arabic RTL throughout. DPDPA compliance embedded in the product, not in a compliance PDF.

This is the product that shouldn't exist if you believe enterprise software should be built globally first and localised later. We think the opposite. The hard version of enterprise software is solved locally first — and then, only if you've actually earned it — expanded globally.

## InsightTest AI, and the testing superpower India hasn't used on itself.

India exports over **eight billion dollars in software testing services every year**<sup class="fn"><a href="#s1">1</a></sup>. We are, by revenue, the largest software-testing economy on Earth. And yet most of that testing still happens the way it happened in 2015: manual test case writing, brittle Selenium scripts, flaky tests that break when a developer renames a CSS class.

Meanwhile, the global frontier has moved. AI-driven self-healing automation cuts test-maintenance effort by thirty to forty percent<sup class="fn"><a href="#s8">8</a></sup>. Modern platforms build regression suites in hours instead of weeks. The gap between a team that has adopted AI testing and one that hasn't is, in real terms, the difference between shipping weekly and shipping monthly.

There are broadly two kinds of companies right now. The ones that have made that leap, and the ones that haven't yet. The first group is small, and mostly concentrated in enterprise contracts with names like Tricentis and Functionize. The second group is everyone else.

[InsightTest AI](/platforms/insighttest) is built for the 90% of Indian engineering teams in the second group. Not for the TCS with 6,500 dedicated QA engineers. For the three-hundred-person SaaS company in Bangalore that's trying to ship to the world with fifteen SDETs and a lot of ambition. Self-healing automation that doesn't need a senior engineer to maintain. AI test generation that lets a product manager contribute a test. A unified platform for mobile, web, API, and analytics — because Indian teams are small and can't afford four separate vendors.

The thesis applied to software engineering: world-class AI testing already exists. It has just existed, up to now, for enterprise buyers with enterprise budgets. Now it needs to exist for the tier-two Indian SaaS at a price — and in a form — that actually works for them.

## One thesis, four times.

Step back from the four products and a single pattern emerges.

A ten-year-old in Patna deserves a mentor who knows her ambitions, in her language, on her phone.

A nineteen-year-old in a state engineering college deserves instruction quality roughly equivalent to what an IIT student gets.

A thirty-two-year-old HR professional deserves an HRMS that doesn't require her to work around the product's American assumptions to do basic Indian compliance.

A twenty-eight-year-old engineer in a growing Indian SaaS deserves the same AI testing tools her global competitors use, at a price that works for her.

These aren't four problems. They are one problem repeated: the best AI-native software in the world has been built with a user in mind who doesn't look like most Indians. It uses English first. It assumes American payroll. It assumes Silicon Valley purchasing power. It assumes a university with proper compute.

> Our thesis is that an Indian-headquartered lab that treats these constraints not as edge cases but as the default will build better products for the 1.4 billion people those global products have been incidentally neglecting.

And, along the way, will probably discover that those same products serve much of the Global South just as well.

Four products, because the life of an Indian learner-worker-builder has four distinct phases. One thesis, because the solve is the same at every phase.

## What we don't know yet.

We registered as a company in 2026. We have more to prove than to claim.

Some of what's on our product pages today is roadmap, not shipping. We mark these honestly in sales conversations. If you're reading this and considering being an early customer, we'd rather you know what has shipped and what is a six-month sprint away than discover it after you sign.

We are dependent on Gemini. The AI layer across most of our products runs on Google's stack. That is a real risk. If the model family diverges from where we need it, or the pricing changes meaningfully, we have to be willing to do hard engineering work to swap backbones. We think we are. We haven't been tested on it yet.

We know most Indian edtech has failed. We've read the post-mortems. We know the gap between what schools will pay and what it costs to build this well. We know the government sales cycle is brutal. Our plan is to pilot with a small number of serious customers in each market, learn hard, and scale only when retention and outcome data are undeniable. If the thesis is wrong somewhere, we'd rather find out with ten customers than a hundred.

We don't know yet if all of this is right. We know it is worth a decade of our lives to try.

## Why now, and why us.

We built Shambhavi because we wanted to work on the unglamorous version of a glamorous idea. "AI for India" as a slogan is everywhere. "AI built for the specific constraints of an Indian student on a five-thousand-rupee phone, who speaks Hindi, whose school has no counsellor, whose parents are anxious, whose teacher has forty other students, and who needs a mentor who can actually listen" — that version is much less common. And much harder. And probably more important.

The company was named for *Shambhavi* — the stylised eye in our logo, a Sanskrit term for the kind of seeing that cuts through the surface. Not surveillance. *Perception.* What we want our products to do, eventually, is to perceive the person using them well enough that each one feels as if it were built specifically for her.

We have a very long way to go. We thought you might want to know what we are trying to do, and why.
